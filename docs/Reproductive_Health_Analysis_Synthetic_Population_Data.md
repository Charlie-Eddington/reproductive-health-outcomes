# Reproductive Health Analysis: Synthea Synthetic Population
### Female Patients Aged 18–45 | Obstetric Outcomes, Complications & Social Determinants

---

## Overview

This analysis explores reproductive health outcomes in a synthetic patient population generated by [Synthea](https://synthetichealth.github.io/synthea/), an open-source synthetic patient generator developed by MITRE. The dataset covers **5,141 female patients aged 18–45** and examines pregnancy outcomes, obstetric complications, miscarriage rates, and social determinants of health (SDOH).

The goal is to demonstrate a reproducible public health analytics pipeline — from raw synthetic EHR data through SQL-based analysis to clinical visualization — while critically evaluating where synthetic data diverges from real-world population statistics.

## Data Pipeline

Raw Synthea CSV exports (`patients.csv`, `conditions.csv`) were loaded into an in-memory SQLite database. Conditions were filtered to clinically relevant SNOMED-CT codes covering:

- **Pregnancy** — normal, high-risk, intrauterine
- **Obstetric complications** — eclampsia, pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, anemia
- **Pregnancy loss** — miscarriage (first and second trimester, complete), blighted ovum, chromosomal abnormality, tubal/ectopic pregnancy
- **Intimate partner violence (IPV)** — abuse and environmental violence codes
- **Social determinants** — employment status, educational attainment, social isolation, housing instability, transportation access

SDOH codes were separated into their own table to allow independent analysis alongside clinical outcomes.

Derived fields added to each patient record:
- `age_group` — banded into 5-year intervals (18–24, 25–29, 30–34, 35–39, 40–45)
- `pregnancy_flag` — any pregnancy-related condition recorded
- `complication_flag` — any obstetric complication recorded
- `miscarriage_flag` — any pregnancy loss recorded
- `ectopic_flag` — tubal/ectopic pregnancy recorded
- `ipv_flag` — intimate partner violence recorded

## Key Findings

### Pregnancy Outcomes by Age Group

Pregnancy rates peak in the 30–34 age group (94.8%) and decline in both younger and older cohorts. Complication rates rise steadily with age, from 32.2% in the 18–24 group to 68.9% in the 40–45 group — consistent with the clinical literature on advanced maternal age. Ectopic pregnancy rates also increase with age (1.2% at 18–24 to 4.2% at 40–45), aligning with known risk factor patterns.

### Miscarriage Rates

Miscarriage rates among pregnant patients rise sharply with age:

| Age Group | Synthea Rate | Benchmark | Source |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| 18–24     | 13.5%       | ~10%      | ACOG, Early Pregnancy Loss (2023) |
| 25–29     | 21.6%       | ~11%      | Magnus et al. (2019), via CDC NSFG |
| 30–34     | 33.6%       | ~15%      | Magnus et al. (2019), via CDC NSFG |
| 35–39     | 48.8%       | ~25%      | Magnus et al. (2019), via CDC NSFG |
| 40–45     | 62.4%       | ~33–53%   | ACOG (2023); Magnus et al. (2019) |

While the age-related trend is directionally correct, Synthea over-generates miscarriage across all age groups. This is a known characteristic of the synthetic population and is discussed further in the limitations section.

### Obstetric Complication Distribution

Anemia is the most prevalent complication (36.3% of pregnant patients), followed by first-trimester miscarriage (20.5%). Eclampsia and pre-eclampsia appear at nearly identical rates (~13%), which is a significant departure from real-world data and is addressed in the data quality section below.

### SDOH Factors in Complicated vs Normal Pregnancies

SDOH factors show minimal differentiation between patients with and without obstetric complications. The largest gaps were observed in employment status — patients not in the labor force appeared at a slightly higher rate in the complicated group (46.4% vs 40.9%). Social isolation and housing instability showed small but directionally consistent differences.

However, the overall uniformity of SDOH distribution across outcome groups limits the utility of this comparison in the synthetic dataset. Real-world data consistently shows stronger associations between social determinants and obstetric outcomes, particularly for low-income and minority populations.

## ⚠️ Data Quality & Limitations

This analysis uses synthetic data and results should not be used to draw clinical or policy conclusions. The following limitations were identified during the analysis:

### Eclampsia & Pre-Eclampsia Over-Representation
The most significant data quality issue in this dataset. Eclampsia and pre-eclampsia appear at nearly equal rates (~13% of pregnant patients each), whereas real-world US data shows a substantial gap:

- **Pre-eclampsia:** 5–7% of pregnancies (CDC, 2022)
- **Eclampsia:** 0.016–0.1% of deliveries (Fishel Bartal & Sibai, AJOG, 2022)

This is a known bug in Synthea's pregnancy module that causes pre-eclampsia diagnoses to be incorrectly escalated to eclampsia at a much higher rate than is clinically accurate.

### Miscarriage Over-Representation
Miscarriage rates are elevated across all age groups compared to CDC/ACOG benchmarks. The synthetic population appears to over-generate pregnancy loss, particularly in older age groups.

### Pregnancy Rate
78% of patients aged 18–45 have at least one pregnancy recorded — higher than population-level estimates. Synthea's pregnancy module generates pregnancies at a rate that does not fully reflect real-world variation in fertility intentions, contraceptive use, or infertility.

### Complication Rate Inflation
The 55% overall complication rate is partly inflated by the inclusion of general anemia (`271737000`), which Synthea applies broadly rather than exclusively in obstetric contexts. A more precise analysis would use obstetric-specific anemia codes only.

### IPV Rate
51% of patients have an IPV-related code recorded, substantially higher than real-world lifetime prevalence estimates of 25–35% (CDC, National Intimate Partner and Sexual Violence Survey). The broad application of environmental violence codes (`424393004`) in Synthea contributes to this inflation.

### SDOH Uniformity
Social determinant codes are applied relatively uniformly across the Synthea population and do not meaningfully correlate with health outcomes the way they do in real-world EHR and claims data. SDOH findings in this analysis should be interpreted as illustrative rather than substantive.

### Infertility Underrepresentation
Only 1 case of female infertility (`707577004`) was identified in the entire cohort. Synthea does not robustly model infertility as a standalone condition in this population.

### No Induced Abortion Codes
No induced abortion SNOMED codes were present in this export. Synthea does not currently model induced abortion as a clinical pathway.
